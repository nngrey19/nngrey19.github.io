HMS Surprise by Patrick O'Brian

March 14, 2021

While each of the novels in the Aubrey–Maturin series challenge the reader with their specialized language and abrupt transitions, this installment seemed especially demanding. We hop from battles at sea to torture houses in Catalan to the streets of Calcutta. There is quite a bit of odd extraneous action, like Maturin visiting an island and nearly getting swept away in a storm and the Ambassador Stanhope dying on the beach of distant island.

One odd aspect of this novel is the portrayal of character named Dil. Dill is an Indian street urchin befriended by Maturin. Whiel she is something of a sympathetic character, her dialogue is completely difficult to bear. I suppose O'Brian was attempting to make her exotic, but she he goes so overboard it is hard to read. She sounds like some sort of gothic demon, not like a child. In general, Dil and her mother are portrayed as barbaric and unnatural. It is a bit disapponting that an author so attuned to detail and subtlty in sailing could be so clueless when it comes to shaping a character from another culture.

I am currently reading The Nickel Boys, Colson Whitehead and Our Man in Havana, Graham Greene.
