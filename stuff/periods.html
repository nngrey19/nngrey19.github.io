The Baroque and Rococo, 1600 - 1750

  • Claudio Monteverdi, 1567 – 1643, transitional figure between Renaissance and Baroque. He wrote many madrigals and developed the Italian operatic style, L'Orfeo (1607) is the earliest opera still widely performed.
  • Johann Sebastian Bach, 1685-1750, employed many forms including concerto grosso, fugue, cantata.
  • George Frideric Handel, 1685-1759, famous for operas and oratorios.
  • Jean-Antoine Watteau (1684-1721): Watteau was a French painter known for his fête galante paintings, which depicted elegant outdoor gatherings of the aristocracy. His works captured the spirit of Rococo with their graceful figures, soft colors, and dreamlike atmosphere.
  • Jean-Honoré Fragonard (1732-1806): Fragonard was a French painter known for his exuberant and vibrant Rococo style. His works, such as "The Swing" and "The Progress of Love" series, showcased his skill in capturing movement, lush landscapes, and romantic themes.
  • The Enlightenment, 1715 - 1789

Classical and Neo-classical, 1750 - 1800

  • Franz Joseph Haydn, 1732-1809, Father of the Symphony and String Quartet
  • Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, 1756-1791
  • Ludwig van Beethoven, 1770-1827 (transitional)
  • Jacques-Louis David (1748-1825): David was a leading French painter and the most prominent Neoclassical artist. He became the official painter of the French Revolution and later Napoleon Bonaparte. David's works, such as "The Death of Socrates" and "Napoleon Crossing the Alps," embodied the ideals of patriotism, heroism, and moral virtue.
  • Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (1780-1867): Ingres, a French painter, embraced the Neoclassical style with a focus on precise draftsmanship, clean lines, and idealized figures. His portraits and historical paintings, such as "The Apotheosis of Homer" and "La Grande Odalisque," showcased his meticulous technique and attention to detail.
