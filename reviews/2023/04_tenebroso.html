Tenebrism, from Italian tenebroso ("dark, gloomy, mysterious"), also occasionally called dramatic illumination, is a style of painting using especially pronounced chiaroscuro, where there are violent contrasts of light and dark, and where darkness becomes a dominating feature of the image. See The Calling of St Matthew.

Chiaroscuro (kee-AR-É™-SKOOR-oh, Italian for 'light-dark'), is the use of strong contrasts between light and dark, usually bold contrasts affecting a whole composition.

What is the difference between tenebrism and chiaroscuro? Tenebrism is used to achieve a spotlight effect. Some areas of the painting are completely black, allowing others to be illuminated, usually from a single source of light. Chiaroscuro is a less extreme contrast of light and shade used to create the illusion of three-dimensional form.

The impact of the reformation and counter-reformation on art.

The Reformation produced two main branches of Protestantism - the Evangelical Lutheran churches, which followed the teachings of Martin Luther, and the Reformed Churches, which followed the ideas of John Calvin and Huldrych Zwingli.

The Reformed Churches removed religious imagery, while the Lutheran churches offered guidance on what types of imagery were appropriate.

The Council of Trent, 1545 - 1563, was a council of the Catholic Church prompted by the Protestant Reformation. It has been described as the embodiment of the Counter-Reformation.

The Council of Trent also offered guidance on what type of imagery was appropiate.
