1870

  • The premiere of Tchaikovsky's fantasy-overture Romeo and Juliet in Moscow. The piece helped define the symphonic poem genre and is closely associated with Shakespearean adaptations in music.

    A symphonic poem (also called a tone poem) is a single-movement orchestral work that tells a story, evokes a scene, or conveys an idea. Though the genre was pioneered by composers like Franz Liszt in the mid-19th century, Tchaikovsky’s Romeo and Juliet became one of its defining examples due to its clear dramatic structure, mirroring the narrative arc of Shakespeare’s play, and the use of leitmotifs (recurring musical themes) to represent different characters and emotions.

    Tchaikovsky was one of the first composers to successfully translate Shakespearean drama into purely instrumental music. His Romeo and Juliet set the standard for later musical adaptations of Shakespeare’s works.
